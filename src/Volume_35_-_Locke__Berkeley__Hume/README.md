# Volume 35 - Locke, Berkeley, Hume

## JOHN LOCKE (1632-1704)

John Locke was an English philosopher and physician, regarded as the father of classical liberalism and one of the most influential Enlightenment thinkers. His work on epistemology, political philosophy, and religious toleration shaped modern democratic thought.

### Works in This Volume

- **A Letter Concerning Toleration** - Locke's argument for religious toleration and the separation of church and state, written during his exile in Holland.

- **Concerning Civil Government, Second Essay** - The second of his *Two Treatises of Government*, presenting his theory of natural rights, government by consent, and the right of revolution. A foundational text of liberal political philosophy.

- **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding** - Locke's masterwork on epistemology, arguing that all knowledge derives from experience. He attacks innate ideas and develops an empiricist account of how the mind acquires and processes ideas.

---

## GEORGE BERKELEY (1685-1753)

Berkeley was an Irish philosopher and Anglican bishop, known for his idealist philosophy. He argued that material objects exist only as perceptions in the mind, famously summarized as *"esse est percipi"* (to be is to be perceived).

### Works in This Volume

- **The Principles of Human Knowledge** - Berkeley's main philosophical work, arguing that matter does not exist independently of perception. All we know are our own ideas, and objects exist only insofar as they are perceived by minds.

---

## DAVID HUME (1711-1776)

Hume was a Scottish philosopher, historian, and essayist, one of the most important figures in Western philosophy. His radical empiricism led him to skeptical conclusions about causation, the self, and religious belief.

### Works in This Volume

- **An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding** - A reworking of ideas from his earlier *Treatise*, presenting Hume's account of causation, the limits of human knowledge, and his famous critique of miracles.
